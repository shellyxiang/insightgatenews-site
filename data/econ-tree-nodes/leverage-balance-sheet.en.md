## Leverage and Balance Sheet Growth

Leverage refers to the use of borrowed funds to increase the size of an asset position or economic activity. In a credit cycle, leverage often rises as borrowers take on more debt relative to their income, capital, or equity base. Banks, firms, households, and investors may all become more leveraged during periods of easy credit. At the same time, balance sheets grow larger as both assets and liabilities expand.

This matters because leverage can magnify both gains and losses. When asset values rise or profits remain strong, leverage can make growth look more powerful and returns appear higher. But when conditions weaken, leverage also makes losses more damaging. A decline in asset prices or income can quickly strain a balance sheet that has too much debt behind it.

Balance-sheet growth is therefore central to understanding why credit cycles can become dangerous. Expansion does not only mean that more lending is happening. It means that more parts of the economy become dependent on continued funding, stable valuations, and favorable conditions. Once this dependence becomes strong enough, even a modest shock can trigger a much wider adjustment.
