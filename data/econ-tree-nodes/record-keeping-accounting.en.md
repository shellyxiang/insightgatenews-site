## Record-Keeping and Accounting

Once obligations extend across time, memory alone is no longer enough. Societies need ways to record who owes what to whom, when payment is due, and what counts as fulfillment. This is why record-keeping and accounting are among the earliest foundations of finance. They turn temporary promises into traceable claims.

Historically, many early systems of economic administration involved recording grain dues, labour services, tax obligations, rents, or merchant claims. This was not merely bureaucratic detail. It made complex economic relationships possible. Once obligations are written down, they can be monitored, enforced, compared, and sometimes transferred. Record-keeping therefore allows credit relations to become more stable and more scalable.

Accounting is important not only because it preserves information, but because it changes the nature of economic order. A recorded obligation is more durable than a remembered one. It can survive distance, delay, and the absence of the original parties. In this sense, record-keeping is one of the mechanisms through which finance moves from personal trust alone toward institutional structure.
