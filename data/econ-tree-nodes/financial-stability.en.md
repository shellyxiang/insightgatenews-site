## Financial Stability and Crisis

Financial stability refers to the condition in which the financial system can continue performing its core functions — allocating credit, processing payments, supporting liquidity, and managing risk — without severe disruption. A crisis occurs when these functions break down or become seriously impaired, often through the interaction of falling asset prices, liquidity stress, funding problems, and collapsing confidence.

This is a dynamic problem because crises rarely come from a single isolated event. They usually emerge when several vulnerabilities reinforce one another. A fall in asset prices may weaken balance sheets. Weaker balance sheets may reduce lending. Reduced lending may depress economic activity. Slower activity may increase defaults, which in turn further weakens the financial system. In this way, local stress can turn into broader instability through feedback loops and contagion.

The importance of financial stability lies in the fact that a modern economy depends heavily on financial intermediation. When the financial system is stable, payments are processed, firms can refinance, governments can borrow, and households can access credit. When stability breaks down, even economically viable actors may be cut off from funding or forced into distress. This is why modern central banking, prudential regulation, lender-of-last-resort policy, and macro-financial analysis all give such importance to crisis prevention and crisis management. Financial stability is not simply the absence of panic. It is the condition that allows the wider economy to function through finance rather than be destabilized by it.
