## Equity Securities

Equity securities are financial instruments that represent ownership claims rather than debt claims. The most familiar form is the share or stock. When an investor buys an equity security, they are not simply lending money and expecting fixed repayment. They are acquiring a claim on the future profits and value of the firm, and often some participation in its governance.

This makes equity fundamentally different from debt. Debt promises fixed obligations, at least in principle. Equity does not. Equity holders are residual claimants, which means they are paid after debt obligations have been met. This gives them more uncertainty, but also access to potential upside if the firm grows, becomes more profitable, or rises in market value.

Equity securities are central to modern capitalism because they allow firms to raise capital without taking on fixed repayment obligations in the same way as debt. They spread ownership across investors, support large-scale enterprise, and create a market-based way of valuing future profits. In this sense, equity securities are one of the key bridges between business activity and financial markets.
