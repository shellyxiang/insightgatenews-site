## Primary Equity Market

The primary equity market is where firms issue new shares to raise capital from investors. This is the stage at which ownership claims are first created and sold. The most widely known example is an initial public offering, or IPO, but firms may also issue additional shares later to raise more capital.

This market matters because it allows firms to obtain funding without borrowing in the form of debt. Instead of promising fixed interest payments, the firm gives investors ownership claims. This can be especially important for firms that want to expand, invest, or take on risk without adding too much repayment pressure to their balance sheets.

In modern finance, the primary equity market plays a major role in business growth, corporate finance, and the expansion of capital markets. It determines which firms can access broad investor funding and on what terms. It also reflects expectations about the firm’s future value, since investors buy equity not for fixed repayment, but for the possibility of future earnings and capital gains. In this way, the primary equity market is where firms turn future potential into present financing.
