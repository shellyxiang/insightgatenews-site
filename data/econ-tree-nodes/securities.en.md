## Securities

Securities are standardized financial claims that can be issued, held, bought, and sold. They are one of the defining features of modern finance because they turn financial relationships into transferable instruments. Instead of keeping every claim locked inside a direct relationship between one lender and one borrower, securities make those claims more comparable, more tradable, and more scalable.

This matters because modern finance depends not only on lending, but on the ability to package claims in ways that many different investors can hold and trade. Governments use securities to borrow. Firms use them to raise capital. Investors use them to allocate savings, manage risk, and seek returns. Markets use them as the raw material for pricing, liquidity, and portfolio construction.

In this sense, securities mark an important shift in financial history. Finance is no longer only about who owes what to whom. It becomes a system in which claims can be standardized, priced, and exchanged across large markets. This is one of the key steps from a simple credit economy to a mature financial system.
