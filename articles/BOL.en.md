# Bolivia: Gas, State Expansion, and a Resource Economy Facing Rising Macroeconomic Stress

Bolivia is a **South American economy whose structure is shaped by natural gas, mining, a large state role, and a development model that has come under increasing macroeconomic stress**. It is not a diversified industrial economy, and it is not a high-income resource state. Its economic role comes from a different combination: hydrocarbons, minerals, a strong redistributive state presence, and an economy whose former resource-supported stability is now weakening as external and fiscal pressures grow.

## 1. Hydrocarbons and Mining Define Bolivia’s External Role

Bolivia’s clearest external identity remains tied to hydrocarbons and mining. Natural gas has historically been central to exports and fiscal revenues, while minerals also remain important. This makes Bolivia one of South America’s clearer resource-centered economies.

## 2. The State Has Been Central to the National Model

A defining feature of Bolivia’s economy is the weight of the state. Public spending, subsidies, redistribution, and state influence have long played major roles in the national development model. This has supported activity and social spending, but it has also made the economy increasingly dependent on fiscal and external resources.

## 3. The Old Resource-Supported Model Is Under Strain

Recent IMF assessments make clear that Bolivia’s macroeconomic outlook under current policies is unsustainable and that the risks of a balance-of-payments and fiscal crisis are rising. Hydrocarbon revenues have weakened, growth has slowed, and the policy framework has become more difficult to sustain. This means Bolivia’s challenge is no longer only about development. It is also about adjustment under stress.

## 4. The Economy Is More Fragile Than Growth Alone Suggests

Bolivia still has activity in agriculture, services, and domestic markets, but these are not enough to fully offset weakening resource performance and rising macroeconomic stress. This gives the economy a fragile profile: it is not collapsed, but the foundations of the previous model are under pressure.

## 5. The Main Structural Question Is Whether Bolivia Can Move Beyond a Weakening State-Led Resource Model

The deeper issue for Bolivia is not whether state support and natural resources can still sustain activity in the short run. They can, to a point. The more important question is whether a resource economy with rising fiscal and external stress can transition toward a more sustainable, diversified, and credible macroeconomic framework. In other words, the challenge is not only growth, but model repair.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Bolivia is best read as a **state-heavy South American resource economy built on gas, mining, and redistribution, but increasingly constrained by weakening external revenues and rising macroeconomic stress**. Its distinct role comes from the interaction of natural-resource dependence, a large public-sector model, and growing balance-of-payments pressure.
