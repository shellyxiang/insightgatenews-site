# Solomon Islands: Agriculture, Gold, and a Small Pacific Economy with Narrow Buffers

Solomon Islands is a **small Pacific island economy whose structure is shaped by agriculture, limited natural resources, aid and public spending, and high vulnerability to external shocks**. It is not a tourism-led economy on the Fiji model, and it is not a high-income state. Its economic role comes from a different combination: agriculture, fisheries, some mining activity, aid-linked public expenditure, and a narrow economy with weak buffers.

## 1. Agriculture and Primary Sectors Remain Central

The Solomon Islands economy still relies heavily on agriculture and other primary sectors. These remain important to livelihoods and production. This means the country’s structure is still closer to a basic primary-economy model than to the service-heavy island economies of the Pacific.

## 2. Gold and Resource Activity Can Meaningfully Affect Growth

Recent growth has been supported by gold production alongside agriculture. This matters because even relatively small shifts in mining output can have large effects in a small economy with limited diversification.

## 3. The Economy Is Narrow and Highly Vulnerable

A defining feature of Solomon Islands is narrowness. Because the economy is small, changes in commodity prices, import costs, and external conditions have outsized effects. This makes vulnerability a structural characteristic rather than just a temporary risk.

## 4. Fiscal and External Buffers Remain Limited

Recent IMF materials note that the current account briefly returned to surplus in 2025, but the fiscal deficit remains wide and external conditions remain fragile. This means that improvements can occur, but the overall policy space is still constrained.

## 5. The Main Structural Question Is Whether a Small Primary Economy Can Build More Resilience

The deeper issue for Solomon Islands is not whether agriculture and some resource activity can continue supporting the economy. They can. The more important question is whether a very small Pacific economy can build stronger buffers, diversify modestly, and reduce vulnerability to external shocks. In other words, the challenge is not only growth, but resilience.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Solomon Islands is best read as a **small primary-sector Pacific economy built on agriculture, some resource activity, aid-linked spending, and weak buffers rather than on tourism or industrial depth**. Its distinct role comes from combining narrow productive activity with high structural vulnerability.
