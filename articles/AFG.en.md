# Afghanistan: Aid Shock, Agriculture, and a Fragile Survival Economy

Afghanistan is a **fragile low-income economy shaped by aid reduction, agriculture, and survival-level adjustment**. It is not a hydrocarbon state, and it does not have the export-manufacturing base seen in Bangladesh or Pakistan. Its role comes from a different combination: a weak formal economy, strong dependence on agriculture and basic services, reduced foreign aid, and repeated pressure from displacement, returnee inflows, and low investment.

## 1. Reduced Aid Has Changed the Structure

Recent World Bank reporting shows that Afghanistan's economy in 2025 was severely affected by reduced foreign aid and regional shocks. This is structurally important because aid used to support large parts of the economy. The reduced aid environment means Afghanistan now operates under a much tighter resource base.

## 2. Agriculture Still Matters Greatly

Agriculture remains central. The World Bank's 2025 update noted a record irrigated wheat harvest despite drought conditions, showing that agriculture still plays a major role in sustaining output and livelihoods. This gives Afghanistan a more agrarian profile than many other Muslim-majority countries in your project.

## 3. Services and Industry Recover in a Fragile Way

The same World Bank materials also say that services and industry have been supporting output growth, partly because more than two million returnees from Iran and Pakistan increased demand. But they also note that this population surge reduces per capita income and puts pressure on jobs and capital. So growth in Afghanistan is fragile and highly constrained.

## 4. Returnees and Displacement Affect the Economy Directly

Afghanistan's economy is shaped not only by production sectors, but also by population movement. Large returnee inflows affect services, labor markets, and household welfare directly. This makes Afghanistan different from more stable economies where migration is a side issue rather than a macroeconomic factor.

## 5. Structural Pressures

Afghanistan's main pressures are severe: low investment, weak formal employment, aid reduction, high poverty risk, and repeated shocks. Even when GDP grows, World Bank materials stress that per capita conditions can still worsen because of rapid population increases and weak capital formation.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Afghanistan is best read as a **fragile agrarian economy under aid shock and survival pressure**. Its distinct role comes not from strong exports or resource rent, but from the difficulty of sustaining livelihoods and basic output under extremely constrained conditions.
