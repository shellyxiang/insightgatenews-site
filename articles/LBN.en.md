# Lebanon: Services, Remittances, and a Small Economy Shaped by Crisis

Lebanon is a **small services-and-remittances economy whose structure has been deeply reshaped by prolonged financial crisis**. It is not a hydrocarbon state, and it is not a manufacturing exporter on the scale of Turkey or Morocco. Its role comes from a different combination: tourism, services, external income from the diaspora, and a financial system that used to define the economy but later became the center of collapse.

## 1. Services and Tourism Are Central

Lebanon's economy has long relied on services. The World Bank's 2025 and 2026 updates describe recovery being supported partly by tourism and consumption, showing that services remain central even after years of crisis. This gives Lebanon a Mediterranean-service profile rather than a resource or manufacturing profile.

## 2. Remittances and the Diaspora Matter Deeply

Lebanon's external position has also been shaped by remittances and diaspora-linked inflows. OECD and IMF-linked discussions of Lebanon's macro structure have long highlighted how non-tax and external income sources helped sustain the economy. This means Lebanon's external earnings depend less on goods exports and more on people, services, and external financial ties.

## 3. The Financial System Was Once a Core Identity

Before the crisis, banking and financial intermediation were central to Lebanon's economic identity. But later analysis shows that the financial system became the core of the collapse itself, with bank resolution and restructuring becoming key policy issues. This makes Lebanon unusual: finance once defined its economic role, but crisis later redefined that role around stabilization and repair.

## 4. Crisis Reshaped the Entire Economy

The World Bank's 2026 Lebanon Economic Monitor describes 2025 as a year of modest recovery after severe contraction, supported by macro stabilization, tourism, and uneven reform progress. That means Lebanon is no longer best described by its old pre-crisis profile alone; the post-crisis condition is now part of the economic structure.

## 5. Structural Pressures

Lebanon's pressures are deeper than ordinary cyclical weakness. They include banking-system repair, public-finance weakness, external fragility, and the difficulty of rebuilding confidence after systemic collapse. These are long-duration constraints, not temporary setbacks.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Lebanon is best read as a **small Levantine services economy shaped by diaspora income and deeply altered by financial crisis**. Its distinct role comes from services, tourism, and remittances, but its current limits come from the unresolved legacy of collapse.
