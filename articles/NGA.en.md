# Nigeria: Scale, Oil Dependence, and an Uneven Large-Market Economy

Nigeria is a **large but uneven economy whose structure is shaped by domestic market scale, oil-dependent fiscal and foreign-exchange earnings, and the growing weight of services alongside persistent poverty and weak broad-based productivity gains**. It is not a small hydrocarbon state, even though oil remains critical. Its economic role comes from a different combination: a very large population, a vast internal market, a state still heavily exposed to oil revenues, expanding services, and a difficult conversion of national scale into broad improvements in living standards. 

## 1. Oil Matters More to the State Than to the Whole Economy

Nigeria is often associated with oil, but the more important point is that oil matters disproportionately to government finance and foreign exchange rather than dominating the whole economy in output terms. The World Bank’s 2025 Nigeria Development Update states that the oil sector is under 10 percent of GDP, yet it still provides the bulk of fiscal revenues and foreign-exchange earnings. That means Nigeria is not a classic Gulf-style oil economy; it is a large mixed economy whose state finances remain unusually exposed to a relatively small sector. 

## 2. Domestic Scale Is One of Nigeria’s Main Economic Assets

Nigeria’s economic weight comes not only from natural resources but also from population and market size. With a population above 240 million according to the IMF, Nigeria is by far the largest market in West Africa, which gives it a regional position that smaller neighboring economies cannot match. This scale helps explain why trade, retail, transport, finance, digital services, and informal economic activity all matter greatly. Nigeria is therefore not just an exporter of oil; it is also a very large internal market economy. 

## 3. Services Are Increasingly Central to Growth

Recent growth in Nigeria has not come only from hydrocarbons. The IMF’s 2025 Article IV materials note that services contributed strongly to growth, while agriculture remained subdued and manufacturing and trade were pressured by naira depreciation and high energy costs. This gives Nigeria a different profile from a narrow commodity exporter: growth increasingly depends on how well services, commerce, and non-oil activity expand, even while macroeconomic stability still depends heavily on oil-linked revenue and foreign exchange. 

## 4. Poverty and Household Pressure Remain Central to the Story

Nigeria’s size should not be confused with broad prosperity. World Bank data show GDP per capita at about $1,084 in 2024, and the World Bank’s 2025 Poverty and Equity Brief projects that 52.5 percent of Nigerians will live in poverty in 2025. This means Nigeria is economically important without being a rich country in per capita terms. The central challenge is not simply how to grow output, but how to make a very large economy generate wider household gains in income, jobs, and purchasing power. 

## 5. Nigeria Is a Regional Heavyweight, but Its Structure Is Internally Uneven

Nigeria should also be read as a West African anchor economy rather than as a standalone national case. Its market size, oil exports, and service-sector expansion give it regional influence, but its internal structure remains uneven. The IMF highlights weak agricultural productivity, security-related constraints, high energy costs, and exchange-rate pressures on manufacturing and trade. So Nigeria’s main economic question is not whether it has scale, but whether it can convert scale into more stable and productive development across a much broader share of the economy. 

## Conclusion

In the global system, Nigeria is best read as a **large-market West African economy whose national weight rests on population, oil-linked state revenues, and expanding services, but whose broader development remains constrained by poverty, uneven productivity, and structural imbalances**. Its distinct role does not come from high average wealth, but from the combination of scale, regional centrality, and the unresolved gap between economic size and broad-based prosperity.
