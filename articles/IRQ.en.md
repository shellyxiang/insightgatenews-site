# Iraq: Oil Dependence, Fiscal Pressure, and a Narrow Non-Oil Base

Iraq is a **hydrocarbon-dependent state whose fiscal and external position remains heavily tied to oil**. It is not a Gulf-style finance platform, and it does not yet have a strong non-oil base capable of redefining the economy. Its role comes from a combination of large oil resources, a state budget strongly linked to energy income, and repeated pressure to expand non-oil revenue and private-sector activity.

## 1. Oil Still Dominates the Model

Oil remains the core of Iraq's economy. The IMF's 2025 Article IV notes that falling oil prices and financing constraints are weighing on activity, while non-oil GDP growth remains weak. That means Iraq's macroeconomic rhythm is still largely set by the oil sector.

## 2. Fiscal Space Depends on Energy Revenue

Iraq's budget position is closely tied to hydrocarbons. IMF reporting emphasizes that declining oil prices aggravate fiscal and external sustainability risks, and that debt stabilization requires additional fiscal adjustment. This shows that oil is not just an export story in Iraq; it is the central support for public finance.

## 3. The Non-Oil Base Remains Too Narrow

Iraq has scope to raise customs duties, excises, and non-oil revenues, but the IMF repeatedly stresses that these areas remain underdeveloped. This leaves the economy with a narrow base outside hydrocarbons. Iraq is therefore different from countries like Turkey or Morocco, where manufacturing or trade diversification plays a larger role.

## 4. Domestic Stability Matters Economically

The IMF notes that Iraq has maintained domestic stability despite regional turmoil, but also points to arrears accumulation and financing constraints. This means politics and state capacity directly affect economic performance, not just indirectly. Iraq's economy is shaped by both oil dependence and governance pressure.

## 5. Structural Pressures

Iraq's key pressures are persistent: oil-price exposure, weak non-oil revenues, fiscal rigidity, financing stress, and the need to build a more sustainable non-hydrocarbon base. These are recurring features of the model rather than temporary disturbances.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Iraq is best read as a **large oil-dependent state whose main economic challenge is turning resource income into a broader and more durable domestic base**. Its distinct role still comes from hydrocarbons, but its main constraint is how little sits underneath them.
