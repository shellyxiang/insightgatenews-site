# Sudan: Agriculture, Conflict, and a Collapsing Economy Under Severe Disruption

Sudan is a **large northeastern African economy whose structure has been overwhelmed by conflict**. It is connected to the Arab and Islamic world through language, history, and regional identity, but its present economic profile is defined much more by war, displacement, agricultural disruption, and institutional breakdown than by a stable growth model. The World Bank's 2025 Sudan Economic Update describes the economy as suffering one of the world's most severe ongoing collapses since the conflict that began in April 2023.

## 1. Agriculture Traditionally Formed the Base

Before the current collapse, Sudan's economy had long rested on agriculture and related rural activity. Britannica describes cultivation and animal husbandry as historically central to the livelihoods of much of the population, with crops such as sorghum, millet, cotton, sesame, and groundnuts playing important roles. This gave Sudan a different base from oil-rich Arab states: it was historically more of an agricultural economy than a hydrocarbon one.

## 2. Conflict Now Dominates the Economic Story

That older structure is no longer enough to explain Sudan's economy. The World Bank's 2025 update says the conflict has caused massive destruction of productive capacity, severe loss of output, and deep humanitarian distress. Global Economic Prospects materials in 2025 also described Sudan as experiencing another year of double-digit contraction because of violent conflict. In other words, today's economic story is shaped less by sectoral structure than by systemic collapse.

## 3. Trade, Finance, and Daily Economic Activity Have Been Disrupted

The effects of conflict run through every layer of the economy. The World Bank notes that disruption has affected agriculture, industry, markets, and basic services, while regional IMF materials point to spillovers into neighboring countries through refugee flows and security pressures. This means Sudan's economy cannot be read as a normal open economy under strain; it is an economy whose internal circulation has been deeply broken.

## 4. A Country with Identity Linkages, but Weak Economic Pull Inside This Module

Sudan still belongs culturally and politically to the Arab–Islamic sphere in important ways. Arabic language, historical ties, and regional institutions keep that linkage visible. But economically, Sudan is less representative of a stable "Muslim-world" growth path than countries such as Egypt, Algeria, or Morocco. Its inclusion in this module is therefore more about regional and civilizational linkage than about a strong standalone economic type.

## 5. The Main Constraint Is Not Diversification but Survival and Reconstruction

For Sudan, the immediate economic issue is not whether the model is diversified enough. It is whether production, trade, institutions, and basic macroeconomic functions can be restored at all. The World Bank's 2025 outlook explicitly tied any recovery to conflict resolution. This makes Sudan different from most other countries in your map: the central question is not optimization, but reconstruction.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Sudan is a **conflict-fractured agricultural economy with Arab–Islamic regional linkages, but weak current structural pull as a standalone Muslim economic type**. It belongs in this module only as a more weakly connected case, because war and collapse dominate the economic story.
