# Iran: Sanctions, Oil, and a Large State-Shaped Economy

Iran is a **large Middle Eastern economy shaped by hydrocarbons, state influence, and long-running sanctions pressure**. It is not a Gulf-style platform economy like the UAE, and it is not a small fragile state like Lebanon or Jordan. Its economic role comes from a different combination: large oil and gas resources, a broad domestic economy, a sizable industrial base, and external constraints that have repeatedly pushed the country toward adaptation under sanctions.

## 1. Oil and Gas Still Matter Deeply

Hydrocarbons remain central to Iran's economy. The World Bank's 2025 macro outlook notes that oil revenues are under pressure from tighter U.S. sanctions, while non-oil revenues and domestic adjustment are increasingly important. Even so, oil and gas still shape fiscal space, external earnings, and overall macro performance.

## 2. Iran Is More Than a Pure Rent State

Unlike some Gulf economies, Iran also has a broad domestic production base. The economy includes manufacturing, services, agriculture, and state-linked industry, which gives it more internal depth than a narrow export-rent model. This is why Iran should not be read simply as "another oil state."

## 3. Sanctions Are a Structural Economic Force

Sanctions are not a temporary side issue in Iran's case; they are part of the economic structure. The World Bank notes that stricter sanctions have weighed on oil revenues, and external analysis in 2026 also highlighted pressure from weaker Chinese demand for Iranian oil. That means Iran's economy is shaped not only by resources, but also by restricted access to markets, finance, and trade channels.

## 4. The State Plays a Larger Role Than in Many Peers

Iran's economy is also more state-shaped than many other Muslim-majority economies in your project. Public institutions, quasi-state actors, and policy controls play a larger role in organizing production, energy revenue, and adjustment. This gives Iran a profile that is closer to a **sanctioned state-capitalist resource economy** than to a liberalized trade platform.

## 5. Structural Pressures

Iran's main pressures are persistent: sanctions, oil-revenue volatility, inflationary pressures, fiscal strain, and limits on external financial integration. These are not short-term fluctuations; they are recurring features of the model.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Iran is best read as a **large sanctioned hydrocarbon economy with a broad domestic base and heavy state influence**. Its distinct role comes from combining energy resources, industrial depth, and long-term adaptation to external restriction.
