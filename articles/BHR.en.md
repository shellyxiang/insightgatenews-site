# Bahrain: Finance, Refining, and a Diversified Gulf Economy Under Fiscal Pressure

Bahrain is best understood as a **more diversified Gulf economy built around finance, refining, and services, but still constrained by public debt and hydrocarbon-linked fiscal pressure**. It is not as oil-dominant as Kuwait or Saudi Arabia, and it is not as platform-centered as the UAE. Its role comes from a different combination: a relatively diversified non-oil economy, a long-established financial sector, refining and industrial activity, and a continued need to manage fiscal vulnerability carefully.

## 1. More Diversified Than Many Gulf Peers

Bahrain is often described as one of the more diversified economies in the GCC. World Bank material notes strengths in heavy industry, financial services, manufacturing, retail, and tourism, and says the non-oil sector accounts for more than 85 percent of GDP. More recent World Bank digital-economy work also highlights financial services, insurance, manufacturing, construction, and government services as key non-oil sectors.

## 2. Finance Is a Core Part of the Identity

Unlike Gulf states whose external image is shaped mainly by oil or gas exports, Bahrain has long been associated with finance. Earlier World Bank GCC work described financial services as Bahrain's largest service subsector, and more recent economic materials continue to present Bahrain as a Gulf economy with an unusually large finance-and-services layer. This is one reason Bahrain stands apart from more purely hydrocarbon-driven states.

## 3. Refining and Industry Still Matter

Diversification does not mean hydrocarbons are irrelevant. Bahrain's refining and industrial base remains important, and World Bank macro outlook material for 2025 noted that the completion of the Sitra refinery upgrade was expected to sharply raise refined oil export volumes. The same World Bank sources also point to aluminum export earnings as an important part of the external picture. So Bahrain is not simply a finance story; it is a mixed economy where finance, refining, and industry all matter.

## 4. Services and Tourism Add Another Layer

Bahrain's external and growth model also includes services. The World Bank expected services exports to rise steadily, led by financial and business services as well as tourism. This gives Bahrain a profile that is broader than a narrow commodity exporter, even though oil-linked revenues still remain fiscally important.

## 5. Fiscal Pressure Is the Main Constraint

Bahrain's biggest structural weakness is not the absence of activity, but the weight of public debt and fiscal vulnerability. The IMF's 2025 Article IV conclusion praised steady growth and diversification efforts, but stressed that high and rising public debt requires sustained medium-term fiscal consolidation. World Bank macro outlook materials also warn that elevated spending and delayed fiscal adjustments pose fiscal and external vulnerabilities.

## 6. A Gulf Economy with Breadth, but Limited Fiscal Room

This is what makes Bahrain distinctive inside the Gulf. It has more non-oil breadth than many peers, but less fiscal room than the stronger hydrocarbon balance-sheet states. Bahrain therefore looks less like a classic rent-finance core and more like a **diversified small Gulf economy that must keep growth, services, and reform moving at the same time**.

## Conclusion

In the global system, Bahrain is best understood as a **financial-and-services Gulf economy with refining and industrial depth, but tighter fiscal limits than the largest hydrocarbon states**. Its distinct role comes from diversification, not from sheer oil scale, and from maintaining relevance through finance, services, and industry under more constrained public finances.
